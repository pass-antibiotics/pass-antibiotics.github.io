Dr Elise Crayton — Research Associate, UCL Centre for Behaviour Change

Doctor Elise Crayton is a Research Associate in Health Psychology based at University College London in the Centre for Behaviour Change.

Elise's research has focused upon the use of evidence synthesis and application of behavioural science methods to design, deliver and evaluate behaviour change interventions. Elise has carried out research across multiple healthcare settings within the NHS, engaging with patient, general public and healthcare professional populations. Her research to date has focused upon anxiety and social support for elderly stroke survivors, medication adherence in stroke survivors, quality improvement of the two-week wait cancer referral pathway and safe stewardship of antimicrobials.

Dr Gillian Forbes — Research Fellow, UCL Centre for Behaviour Change

Doctor Gillian Forbes is a Research Fellow based at the UCL Centre of Behaviour Change. Her first degree is in Psychology (University of Dundee) and her MPhil is in Health Psychology (University of St Andrews). She was awarded her PhD in Improvement Science funded by the Health Foundation at the University of Dundee in 2019.

Gillian’s current research focuses on healthcare professional behaviour associated with antibiotic stewardship and the antibiotic seeking behaviour of the general public.

Gillian is interested in the implementation of research evidence into practice and the associated use of behaviour change theory to underpin the design and development of interventions aiming to change clinical practice.

Dr Martin J. Gill — Consultant Medical Microbiologist, Queen Elizabeth Hospital Birmingham

Dr Martin Gill is an NHS Microbiologist in secondary/tertiary hospital with a wide range of specialist adult services. He advises on the diagnosis, management (including the use of antimicrobials) and prevention of infection. He works with a range of healthcare professionals who have responsibility for prescribing and overseeing prescribing. He has chaired the Trust’s Antimicrobial Steering Group and been its Infection Control Doctor. He has research interests in surgical site prevention, antimicrobial resistance and in infection informatics. He collaborates on projects funded by National Institute of Health Research, Economic and Social Research Council.

Dr Arnoupe Jhass — NIHR In Practice Fellow, UCL Institute of Epidemiology and Health Care

Doctor Arnoupe Jhass is an NIHR In Practice Fellow at the Institute for Health Informatics and the Research Department of Primary Care & Population Health, University College London. He undertook his medical training at Southampton, and holds a degrees in health policy from the London School of Economics and the University of Edinburgh.

His research interest is in the use of antibiotics in primary care looking at long-term care facilities in particular. Prior to this fellowship, he held a School of Primary Care Research Grant from the National Institute of Health Research.

Alongside his academic fellowship, Arnoupe works as GP in North London.

Dr Emma Richardson — Research Fellow, Aston University

Emma Richardson is a Research Fellow in Forensic Linguistics at the Aston Institute for Forensic Linguistics, Aston University. She is a qualitative researcher with interests in how policy and guidance documents are used by people in practice, to assist the most vulnerable in society.

Emma’s research activity is largely based within forensic and health and social care settings. She spent 4 years, first at the University of Liverpool and then at the University of Manchester examining the impact of the Care Quality Commission’s (CQC) inspection and rating of health and social care services in England before joining the PASS team at the University of Leicester as an ethnographer.

Now based at Aston, she uses conversation analysis to examine investigative interviews with vulnerable adults.

Mr Patrick Rockenschaub — Research Assistant, UCL Institute of Health Informatics

Patrick is a PhD student in Health Data Science at the UCL Institute of Health Informatics and a research assistant for the PASS project. Patrick is responsible for the analysis of the primary care data obtained from large English healthcare databases.

Within the broader remit of his PhD, Patrick is looking into using these real-world medical datasets to optimise the use of antibiotics across healthcare settings using predictive modelling and machine learning.

Dr Catherine Smith — Research Associate, UCL Institute of Health Informatics

Catherine Smith is a postdoctoral infectious disease epidemiologist. In the PASS project, Catherine is leading the Bug Watch community cohort study and the analysis of data from care homes. She has experience in research about the application of novel technologies to improving control of infectious diseases. This has included a randomised controlled trial assessing the use of a mobile app to support treatment of tuberculosis, and a study combining epidemiological information with whole genome sequencing to improve understanding of transmission of viruses in hospitals.
